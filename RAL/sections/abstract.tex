Robots operating in human-populated environments must navigate safely while also respecting social norms, particularly those associated with human group behavior. Most existing navigation approaches primarily focus on avoiding individual humans and often ignore group structures, which can result in socially disruptive behavior despite maintaining collision-free motion. This paper presents TAGA, a modular and lightweight framework for socially aware robot navigation that explicitly accounts for human group formations. TAGA detects groups online using motion-based cues and applies a tangent-based action strategy that guides the robot smoothly around group boundaries while maintaining efficient progress toward the goal. To balance social compliance with physical safety, a hierarchical safety controller is introduced to coordinate group-level avoidance with individual collision prevention. We further propose the Group Collision Rate as a continuous metric to quantify violations of group boundaries during navigation. Extensive simulation experiments across dense, mixed, and dynamic crowd scenarios show that TAGA reduces group boundary intrusions by up to 58 percent while improving navigation success rates from 78 percent to 82 percent and reducing overall collision rates from 20 percent to 16 percent. These results demonstrate that TAGA can be seamlessly integrated with both geometric and learning-based navigation methods, providing an effective and practical solution for socially compliant robot navigation in real-world environments.
