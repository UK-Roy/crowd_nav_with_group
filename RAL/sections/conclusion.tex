% 
This paper presented TAGA, a modular group-aware navigation framework that detects human groups online and computes tangent trajectories around their boundaries without requiring retraining of the base navigation policy. We introduced a five-phase realistic crowd simulation benchmark grounded in empirical literature, and the Group Collision Rate (GCR) metric for continuous social compliance assessment.

Our central empirical finding is a \textit{reactive--learning asymmetry}: explicit geometric group-awareness provides the largest performance gains for reactive baselines (ORCA, SF) that have no internal model of group dynamics, while the benefit diminishes for learning-based policies (DS-RNN, AG-RL) that have implicitly absorbed group-avoidance behavior through training, and is least relevant for group-reward-trained policies (GARN) where group-awareness is already a training objective. This asymmetry provides actionable guidance: TAGA is the right choice when retraining is not feasible; end-to-end training with group rewards is preferable when it is.

Limitations and future directions: (1) TAGA's reactive design cannot anticipate group movement — integrating trajectory prediction would reduce unnecessary detours when groups are moving away from the robot's path. (2) All evaluations are conducted in simulation; real-world deployment with a physical robot is the critical next step. (3) The benchmark currently uses homogeneous sensor models; extending to realistic noisy perception would strengthen the evaluation.
